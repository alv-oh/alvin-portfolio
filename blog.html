What building AirtimeX taught me before I had to pause it

AirtimeX is currently on pause. I want to be upfront about that, because I think there's more to learn from an honest account of why a project stalls than from a tidy success story.

The idea was straightforward: let Safaricom users convert spare airtime to M-Pesa mobile money. I got the core stack running — React Native frontend, Node.js and Express backend, PostgreSQL database, Daraja API integration in the sandbox environment. The basic conversion flow worked in testing. Then coursework picked up, the semester got heavier, and the project went into a folder I keep meaning to reopen.

What I actually learned before pausing: the Daraja API is genuinely tricky. Getting the STK push callbacks right, handling partial transaction failures, making sure money doesn't disappear between the airtime side and the M-Pesa confirmation — none of that is simple, and I hadn't thought carefully enough about error handling when I started. That's the real lesson. Not "shipping is hard" in the abstract, but specifically: anything that touches money needs rollback logic and retry handling before it touches real users, not after.

I'll pick it back up. The repo is still there, the architecture still makes sense, and the problem is still real. Right now it's just waiting for a semester with more hours in it.

AI in Africa isn't a niche — it's the main event

There's a version of the AI conversation that treats Africa as a laggard — a continent that will eventually "catch up" to wherever Silicon Valley currently is. That framing gets everything backwards.

Africa has the youngest population on earth, the fastest-growing mobile internet adoption, and a set of problems — logistics, agriculture, healthcare, financial inclusion — that are genuinely hard and genuinely unsolved. These are exactly the problems where AI has the most to contribute, not the ones where it's marginal (recommending the next Netflix show, optimising ad spend for someone who already has money).

The gap isn't in potential. It's in training data. Most large language models were trained on English-dominant internet text, which means they perform worse on Swahili, Sheng, Yoruba, Zulu — the actual languages people in this continent think and speak in. A model that can't understand how people actually communicate can't solve their actual problems.

What interests me is building at this layer — not fine-tuning GPT-4 for an African use case, but thinking about what data collection, model design, and deployment look like when the user is in Kibera or Kano, on a 2G connection, in a language the model barely knows. That's a harder and more interesting problem than most of what gets called "AI research" in the mainstream.

I don't have the answers yet. I'm a second-year student. But I'm paying attention, and I think the people who solve this will build some of the most important technology of the next decade.

Early notes toward an essay on Afrobeats and the Griot tradition

This isn't a finished essay. It's more like notes I keep coming back to — an argument I'm still working out, that I'm putting here partly to hold myself accountable to actually finishing it.

The central idea: Afrobeats didn't come from nowhere. The global success of artists like Wizkid is downstream of something much older — the Griot tradition of West Africa, where music was never entertainment separate from life but was embedded in everything: work, ceremony, grief, memory. The Griot was a living archive, passing history forward through rhythm and song before there was writing to do it.

I've been reading J.H. Kwabena Nketia's work on African music, and one thing keeps sitting with me: the continuity. The drum as communication. The idea that a song can hold an entire community's identity — not as metaphor, but as actual function. When I listen to "Ojuelegba" I think about that. A neighbourhood compressed into three minutes. That's not a new idea dressed in new sounds. That's inheritance.

What I want to argue, eventually, is that global consumption of Afrobeats isn't the same as global understanding of it. Streams don't equal comprehension. But I haven't figured out how to make that argument properly yet. These are early notes. I'll write the real version when I'm ready.
